# 01, Brand identity foundations

> Identity is what the market says when your name comes up, not what you put on a slide.

## Core thesis

A company's identity is built in **four layers, in this order**:

1. **WHO** it's for, a specific wedge customer (not "businesses").
2. **WHAT** you uniquely do, the outcome you sell, in their language.
3. **HOW** you work, the method that's hard to copy because it's a *way of operating*.
4. **HOW IT SHOWS UP**, voice, visual identity, artifacts, the way you run a call. **Downstream** of 1–3.

The most common failure: jumping to layer 4 (logo, colors, website) before nailing 1–3. The result is a pretty firm nobody can describe in one sentence.

For DS2, layer 3 is **unusually strong** (Challenge-First Delivery is a real method, not a slogan, see `docs/brand/POSITIONING.md`). Layer 1 (the wedge) is weak. That's the gap.

## The canon (read in this order)

### Tier 1, must-internalize

**Marty Neumeier, *The Brand Gap* (2003), *Zag* (2006), *The Brand Flip* (2015).**
Identity is the *gap* between business strategy and customer experience, and it's closed by a few high-craft moves, not by a brand book. Neumeier's **"Onlyness" statement** is the single most useful exercise for a small firm:
> *"DS2 is the **only** ____ that ____ for ____ in the ____ category."*
If you can't finish that sentence, you don't have a position. Apply this in [03-wedge-and-positioning.md](03-wedge-and-positioning.md).

**April Dunford, *Obviously Awesome* (2019), *Sales Pitch* (2023).**
Positioning is a deliberate act with **5 components**:
1. **Competitive alternatives**, what would the customer pick if you didn't exist? (Often "doing nothing" or "internal hire," not a competitor.)
2. **Unique attributes**, what only you have.
3. **Value (and proof)**, what those attributes enable.
4. **Best-fit customers**, who feels the value most acutely.
5. **Market category**, the frame of reference you want to be judged in.
The book is short, practical, and 90% of what you need. *Sales Pitch* extends it into how you actually deliver positioning in a sales call.

**Al Ries & Jack Trout, *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind* (1981).**
The original. Mind-share over market-share. Be first in a category, or invent a new one. Old but the laws still hold.

### Tier 2, systems-level

**Wally Olins, *On B(r)and* (2003), *The Brand Handbook* (2008).**
Identity = **behavior + communication + product + environment**. The systems view, every touchpoint signals identity, including invoices, error messages, and how you handle bad news. DS2's "challenge-first" should show up *across all four channels*, not just on the homepage.

**Christopher Lochhead / Al Ramadan / Dave Peterson, *Play Bigger* (2016).**
**Category design**: the highest-leverage move isn't winning a category, it's creating one. Apple ("smartphone"), HubSpot ("inbound marketing"), Salesforce ("no software"). For DS2: could *Challenge-First Delivery* be a named category, not just a method?

**Geoffrey Moore, *Crossing the Chasm* (1991, updated 2014).**
Useful for the wedge conversation: pick a niche where you can become the *de facto* choice, then expand. The bowling-pin model.

### Tier 3, visual & expression (only after layers 1–3 are nailed)

**Michael Bierut / Pentagram, *How To* (2015).**
Case studies from the world's most respected identity practice. Read for the *thinking*, not the visuals. Particularly: the New School, Hillary 2016 (H+arrow), MIT Media Lab.

**Brian Collins / COLLINS NYC, interviews, talks, the *Brand World* essay.**
The contemporary master. Identity = a **brand world**, not a logo. A universe a customer can step into. Look at Spotify's brand work, Twitch's rebrand, Coca-Cola's recent system.

**Sagmeister & Walsh, *Beauty* (2018) + portfolio.**
Counter-point to "rigid systems." Identity can be expressive, varied, and still coherent. Argues craft + beauty as commercial advantages (research-backed).

**Aaron Draplin, *Pretty Much Everything* (2016).**
Solo-practitioner energy. Antidote to design-by-committee. Useful for a two-founder consultancy in how *not* to overthink visual identity.

## Frameworks DS2 should test itself against

### The Onlyness sentence (Neumeier)
Write it 10 ways. Eliminate the weak ones. The one that survives is your wedge.
> *Current draft to react to: "DS2 is the only senior-led digital partner that challenges the brief before building it for funded-but-pre-team startups."* (Imperfect, refine in [03](03-wedge-and-positioning.md).)

### Dunford's 5 components
For each candidate wedge:
- What are the *actual* competitive alternatives? (Be honest, usually it's "internal team" or "do nothing.")
- What unique attributes does DS2 have? (Challenge-first method, senior-only delivery, Athens-London asymmetry, end-to-end + Stewardship.)
- What's the *value* of those attributes, the outcome the customer feels?
- Who feels that value most acutely?
- What category should DS2 ask to be judged in?

### The 7-second test
Hit the homepage of every candidate wedge framing. Within 7 seconds, can a stranger say:
- What you do
- Who you do it for
- Why you're different
If not, the homepage is wrong, or the positioning is.

### Olins' four-channel audit
For each candidate identity, list the signal in each of the four channels:
| Channel | Signal of "Challenge-First" |
|---|---|
| **Behavior** | Founder pushes back on a bad brief in the first call. |
| **Communication** | "This creates risk because…" wording across every touchpoint. |
| **Product** | A delivery checklist that lists *what we ripped out* alongside what we shipped. |
| **Environment** | Sales calls open with diagnostic questions, not capability decks. |

## How DS2 should apply this

1. **Run the Onlyness exercise**, five drafts, react in a working session. Pick one.
2. **Run Dunford's 5 components** against the chosen wedge. Brutally honest. (See [03](03-wedge-and-positioning.md) for templates.)
3. **Audit the current site** against the 7-second test. (Honest answer right now: it shows craft, but doesn't pass the "who specifically" gate.)
4. **Inventory the Olins four channels**, make "challenge-first" show up in all four, not just copy.
5. **Decide on category**, am I a "consultancy," a "boutique studio," a "venture partner," a "build-with-you firm"? Category drives expectation, pricing, and proof.
